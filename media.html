Best Sellers

Robert Hare's motivation for writing Without Conscience: The Disturbing World of the Psychopaths Among Us stemmed from his extensive work in prisons and his deep concern over the public's and criminal justice system's limited understanding of psychopathy. In the preface, Hare reflected on the public's exposure to criminal behavior, often glamorized or misunderstood in media portrayals, and the real-world impact of psychopathic individuals who leave trails of harm in personal and professional contexts​.

Work with Inmates in Prisons
Hare's early professional experience in prison environments provided him firsthand exposure to individuals who displayed extreme psychopathic traits. During his time at the British Columbia Penitentiary, Hare encountered inmates who exemplified the manipulative, remorseless, and calculated behaviors characteristic of psychopathy. He recounted being unsettled and baffled by their lack of emotional connection and the ease with which they recounted predatory actions. These interactions sparked his fascination and eventual lifelong dedication to understanding and assessing psychopathy​​.

Bringing Science to the Public
Hare aimed to bridge the gap between technical scientific research and public understanding. He recognized that the consequences of misunderstanding psychopathy were dire—not only for the victims of these individuals but also for policymakers and practitioners in the criminal justice system. Hare's work in prisons often involved assessing and understanding behaviors that were not necessarily criminal but deeply antisocial and harmful, emphasizing how psychopathy extended beyond legal definitions of crime​​.

Using Stories to Illustrate Psychopathy
Hare used real-world examples, often drawn from his professional experiences, to illustrate the destructive potential of psychopathy. He noted that many of these individuals could manipulate their environment and people around them with a disarming charm, making it imperative to educate the public and criminal justice professionals on identifying and mitigating their impact. This approach made his work accessible and practical for a broader audience​​.

This book serves as a guide to understanding psychopathy's profound impact and offers insights for protection and awareness in dealing with such individuals.

Robert Hare wrote Snakes in Suits: When Psychopaths Go to Work in collaboration with Paul Babiak to explore psychopathy in corporate settings—a stark departure from its typical association with criminality. This collaboration merged Hare's expertise in psychopathy with Babiak's insights into organizational psychology, creating a nuanced analysis of how psychopathic traits manifest and thrive in the professional world.

Why the Book?
Hare and Babiak recognized a growing need to address the increasing prevalence of corporate malfeasance and the role psychopathic individuals play in such environments. While psychopaths are often linked to crime and violence, Hare and Babiak identified that their traits—charm, manipulation, deceit, and lack of remorse—are equally dangerous in workplaces where they exploit hierarchies and systems for personal gain.

The Collaboration
Paul Babiak brought years of experience in studying organizational behavior and workplace dynamics. Together, they developed the "B-Scan" tool to measure psychopathy in workplace settings, a groundbreaking effort to apply psychopathy research beyond criminal contexts. Their joint work examined how psychopathic individuals maneuver within corporate environments, charming superiors while sabotaging colleagues, creating a “snakes in suits” analogy that resonated widely​​.

Unique Format
The book alternates between analytical chapters explaining psychopathy’s traits and tactics in professional settings, and narrative chapters that follow "Dave," a fictional corporate psychopath. This narrative approach vividly illustrates how a psychopath might manipulate colleagues, exploit workplace systems, and charm superiors, providing readers with a relatable, story-driven lens to understand the real-world application of the book's insights. By combining science with storytelling, Snakes in Suits effectively educates while engaging readers, making complex psychological concepts accessible and memorable.

Recommended Reading

Robert Hare has praised Kent Kiehl's contributions to the study of psychopathy, particularly highlighting the groundbreaking use of neuroimaging to explore the neurobiological underpinnings of the condition. Hare noted that Kiehl's work, including The Psychopath Whisperer: The Science of Those Without Conscience, represents a significant advance in understanding the complex interplay between brain structures and psychopathic behavior.

Hare has specifically recognized Kiehl's innovative research, particularly his findings on the role of the paralimbic system—a network associated with emotional regulation and decision-making—in psychopathy. Interviews

Nicole Kidman’s portrayal of Tracy Safian in Malice exemplifies key psychopathic traits identified by Robert Hare, such as manipulation, deceit, and callousness. Her character’s calculated actions and lack of remorse align with Hare's Psychopathy Checklist-Revised (PCL-R), offering a compelling depiction of a high-functioning individual exploiting others for personal gain.

Spotting a psychopath isn't as straightforward as people assume. Psychopaths are often charming, manipulative, and skilled at blending in. They don’t always fit the stereotypical image of a criminal; instead, their traits—such as a lack of empathy, deceit, and callousness—are often hidden behind a façade of normalcy. Understanding psychopathy requires careful observation of consistent behavioral patterns over time, rather than relying on first impressions.

Psychopathy Scales

PCL-R 2nd Edition

A review of the PCL-R by S.M. Fulero in the Twelfth Mental Measurements Yearbook (pages 453-454), J.C. Conoley and J.C. Impara (eds). Lincoln, Nebraska, Buros Institute, described the PCL-R as "state of the art...both clinically and in research use". Recent surveys of the literature have determined that the PCL-R is the single best predictor of violent behaviour currently available.

In clinical settings, the PCL-R is used for psycho-diagnostic purposes. Because an individual's scores may have important consequences for his or her future, the absolute value is of critical importance. The potential for harm is considerable if the PCL-R is used incorrectly, or if the user is not familiar with the clinical and empirical literature pertaining to psychopathy. Clinicians should:

  • Possess an advanced degree in the social, medical, or behavioral sciences, such as a Ph.D., D.Ed. or M.D.
  • Be registered with the local state or provincial registration body that regulates the assessment and diagnosis of mental disorder (e.g., psychological or psychiatric association);
  • Have experience with forensic populations (as demonstrated by registration as a diploma in forensic psychology or psychiatry, completion of a practicum or internship in a clinical-forensic setting,or at least two years of relevant work-related experience);
  • Limit their use of the PCL-R to those populations in which it has been fully validated. The manual, published in 1991, stated that this meant only adult male forensic populations (e.g.,institutional or community correctional facilities, forensic psychiatric hospitals, and pre trial evaluation or detention facilities.) However, there now is enough empirical evidence to support its use with female and adolescent offenders, as well as with sex offenders.
  • Insure that they have adequate training and experience in the use of the PCL-R. We further recommend that, wherever possible, the PCL-R scores of two independent raters should be averaged so as to increase the reliability of the assessment.

For information on the potential misuse of the PCL-R see:

Hare, R. D. (1998). The Hare PCL-R: Some issues concerning its use and misuse. Legal and Criminological Psychology, 3, 101-122.

PCL:SV Pyschopathy Checklist Screening Version

The original Hare Psychopathy Checklist Revised (PCL-R) has become the standard assessment measure for forensic and correctional populations. However, many professionals requested a brief instrument that has high validity and high reliability ratings similar to the complete Hare PCL-R. The Hare PCL:SV was not designed to replace the PCL-R, but to offer a tool to screen for the possible presence of psychopathy. The Hare PCL:SV is a 12-item scale based on a subset of PCL-R items that can be completed in civic and forensic settings in under one and a half hours. In civic settings, the PCL:SV can be used in psychiatric evaluations, personnel selection, and community studies. Cutoff scores indicate when to follow up the screener with the complete Hare PCL-R, so it is easy to integrate the two instruments for ultimate accuracy.

The PCL:SV was developed for the MacArthur Violence Risk Assessment Study. For updates on the project, as well as a list of publications related to the Study, please visit the MacArthur Research Network web site.

The PCL:SV can be obtained through the publisher Multi-Health Systems

Hare P-Scan: Research Version

The P-Scan provides a useful tool for developing a hypothesis for a particular person of interest, with respect to Psychopathy, and for managing risk for antisocial, criminal, and violent behavior.

The Hare P-Scan: Research Version is a nonclinical tool that serves as a rough screen for psychopathic features and as a source of working hypotheses to deal with or manage suspects, offenders, or clients. It is designed for use in law enforcement, probations, corrections, civil and forensic facilities, and other areas in which it would be useful to have some information about the possible presence of psychopathic features in a person of interest.

The PCL:SV can be obtained through the publisher Multi-Health Systems

Hare Psychopathy Checklist: Youth Version

Identifying youth with psychopathic traits is critical to understanding the factors that contribute to the development of adult psychopathy. The Hare Psychopathy Checklist: Youth Version (PCL:YV) is a 20-item rating scale for the assessment of psychopathic traits in male and female offenders aged 12 to 18.

Adapted from the Hare Psychopathy Checklist-Revised (PCL-R), the most widely used measure of psychopathy in adults, the PCL:YV uses an expert-rater format that emphasizes the need for multidomain and multisource information. Using a semistructured interview and collateral information, the PCL:YV measures interpersonal, affective, and behavioral features related to a widely understood, traditional concept of psychopathy. The PCL:YV yields dimensional scores for clinical purposes, but it can also be used to classify individuals into groups for research purposes.

The PCL:YV can be obtained through the publisher Multi-Health Systems

Antisocial Process Screening Device

The Antisocial Process Screening Device (authors P. J. Frick and R. D. Hare) detects antisocial processes in young populations so that preventative measures can be taken before tendencies lead to crime and other destructive behaviors. Based on the highly popular Hare Psychopathy Checklist-Revised, the APSD screens for Antisocial Personality Disorder or psychopathy. The child is rated on a dimensional scale that probes the characteristic psychopathic pattern of interpersonal, affective, and behavioral symptoms. In addition to the parent (APSD–P) and teacher (APSD–T) forms, a combined form (APSD–C) allows you to integrate and reconcile the responses of multiple informants.

Guidelines for a Psychopathy Treatment Program

The Guidelines for a Psychopathy Treatment Program, by Steve Wong, Ph.D. & Robert D. Hare, Ph.D., focus on adult and adolescent psychopathic offenders. Rather than attempting to modify personality characteristics, the proposed treatment is a strategy of self-management that helps the participant develop a prosocial lifestyle, reducing the frequency and the extent of violent behavior.

The Guidelines for a Psychopathy Treatment Program are prescriptive in nature. They enable participants to uncover the idiosyncratic factors that caused their violent acts, while helping them learn specific preventive skills. At the end of the treatment program, the participant uses a relapse prevention plan to reassemble the new skills, attitudes, and behaviors into a new integrated whole, linking these new attributes in to what will be his or her everyday prosocial functioning.

Self-Report Psychopathy Scale-Fourth Edition

From the MHS web site: "The SRP:4 is the only official self-report version of the world-renowned Psychopathy Checklist-Revised. It is based on a four-factor structure and three large reference samples. The SRP has good reliability and correlates well with PCL-R scores, criminal behavior, academic cheating, sexual promiscuity, peer ratings, aggression, vengeance, stalking and many facets of psychopathy."

The Self-Report Psychopathy Scale-Fourth Edition can by obtained through the publisher, Multi-Health Systems.
