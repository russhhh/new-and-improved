Robert Hare's motivation for writing Without Conscience: The Disturbing World of the Psychopaths Among Us stemmed from his extensive work in prisons and his deep concern over the public's and criminal justice system's limited understanding of psychopathy. In the preface, Hare reflected on the public's exposure to criminal behavior, often glamorized or misunderstood in media portrayals, and the real-world impact of psychopathic individuals who leave trails of harm in personal and professional contexts​.

Work with Inmates in Prisons
Hare's early professional experience in prison environments provided him firsthand exposure to individuals who displayed extreme psychopathic traits. During his time at the British Columbia Penitentiary, Hare encountered inmates who exemplified the manipulative, remorseless, and calculated behaviors characteristic of psychopathy. He recounted being unsettled and baffled by their lack of emotional connection and the ease with which they recounted predatory actions. These interactions sparked his fascination and eventual lifelong dedication to understanding and assessing psychopathy​​.

Bringing Science to the Public
Hare aimed to bridge the gap between technical scientific research and public understanding. He recognized that the consequences of misunderstanding psychopathy were dire—not only for the victims of these individuals but also for policymakers and practitioners in the criminal justice system. Hare's work in prisons often involved assessing and understanding behaviors that were not necessarily criminal but deeply antisocial and harmful, emphasizing how psychopathy extended beyond legal definitions of crime​​.

Using Stories to Illustrate Psychopathy
Hare used real-world examples, often drawn from his professional experiences, to illustrate the destructive potential of psychopathy. He noted that many of these individuals could manipulate their environment and people around them with a disarming charm, making it imperative to educate the public and criminal justice professionals on identifying and mitigating their impact. This approach made his work accessible and practical for a broader audience​​.

This book serves as a guide to understanding psychopathy's profound impact and offers insights for protection and awareness in dealing with such individuals.

Robert Hare wrote Snakes in Suits: When Psychopaths Go to Work in collaboration with Paul Babiak to explore psychopathy in corporate settings—a stark departure from its typical association with criminality. This collaboration merged Hare's expertise in psychopathy with Babiak's insights into organizational psychology, creating a nuanced analysis of how psychopathic traits manifest and thrive in the professional world.

Why the Book?
Hare and Babiak recognized a growing need to address the increasing prevalence of corporate malfeasance and the role psychopathic individuals play in such environments. While psychopaths are often linked to crime and violence, Hare and Babiak identified that their traits—charm, manipulation, deceit, and lack of remorse—are equally dangerous in workplaces where they exploit hierarchies and systems for personal gain.

The Collaboration
Paul Babiak brought years of experience in studying organizational behavior and workplace dynamics. Together, they developed the "B-Scan" tool to measure psychopathy in workplace settings, a groundbreaking effort to apply psychopathy research beyond criminal contexts. Their joint work examined how psychopathic individuals maneuver within corporate environments, charming superiors while sabotaging colleagues, creating a “snakes in suits” analogy that resonated widely​​.

Unique Format
The book alternates between analytical chapters explaining psychopathy’s traits and tactics in professional settings, and narrative chapters that follow "Dave," a fictional corporate psychopath. This narrative approach vividly illustrates how a psychopath might manipulate colleagues, exploit workplace systems, and charm superiors, providing readers with a relatable, story-driven lens to understand the real-world application of the book's insights. By combining science with storytelling, Snakes in Suits effectively educates while engaging readers, making complex psychological concepts accessible and memorable.

Recommended Reading

Robert Hare has praised Kent Kiehl's contributions to the study of psychopathy, particularly highlighting the groundbreaking use of neuroimaging to explore the neurobiological underpinnings of the condition. Hare noted that Kiehl's work, including The Psychopath Whisperer: The Science of Those Without Conscience, represents a significant advance in understanding the complex interplay between brain structures and psychopathic behavior.

Hare has specifically recognized Kiehl's innovative research, particularly his findings on the role of the paralimbic system—a network associated with emotional regulation and decision-making—in psychopathy. Nicole Kidman’s portrayal of Tracy Safian in Malice exemplifies key psychopathic traits identified by Robert Hare, such as manipulation, deceit, and callousness. Her character’s calculated actions and lack of remorse align with Hare's Psychopathy Checklist-Revised (PCL-R), offering a compelling depiction of a high-functioning individual exploiting others for personal gain.

Spotting a psychopath isn't as straightforward as people assume. Psychopaths are often charming, manipulative, and skilled at blending in. They don’t always fit the stereotypical image of a criminal; instead, their traits—such as a lack of empathy, deceit, and callousness—are often hidden behind a façade of normalcy. Understanding psychopathy requires careful observation of consistent behavioral patterns over time, rather than relying on first impressions.
